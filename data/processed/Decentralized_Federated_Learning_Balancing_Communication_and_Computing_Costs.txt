**I. Introduction**
The paper addresses the challenges of centralized Federated Learning (FL), such as single points of failure and high communication costs at the central server. To solve this, the authors propose a **Decentralized Federated Learning (DFL) framework that removes the central server and allows edge clients to exchange models directly with neighbors**. Because existing decentralized methods fail to balance communication efficiency and model consensus jointly, the proposed DFL framework introduces a strategy where each node executes **multiple local updates and multiple inter-node communications** periodically. The authors also introduce a compressed communication scheme (C-DFL) to further reduce communication overhead. 

**II. System Model**
This section defines the mathematical setup of the decentralized network, consisting of $N$ edge nodes with their own distributed, non-overlapping datasets. The primary objective of DFL is to **find optimal model parameters that minimize the global loss function across all distributed data**. Because exact solutions are intractable, the nodes collaboratively solve this using gradient-based local updates based on mini-batches and parallel model averaging with neighboring nodes.

**III. Design of DFL**
The core learning strategy involves two alternating stages: local update and inter-node communication. The framework is controlled by two main variables: **the computation frequency ($\tau_1$) and the communication frequency ($\tau_2$)**. The authors demonstrate that prior algorithms are simply special cases of DFL: D-SGD performs one local update and one communication, while C-SGD performs multiple local updates but only one communication. The authors mathematically combine these update rules into a unified matrix form to facilitate further analysis.

**IV. Convergence Analysis**
The paper establishes **strong convergence guarantees for DFL without assuming convex objectives**. The theoretical convergence upper bound consists of two parts: the original error bound led by synchronous SGD, and the error bound caused by local drift. The analysis reveals three critical impacts on performance:
* **Computation frequency ($\tau_1$):** Increasing local updates intensifies local drift, worsening the bound.
* **Communication frequency ($\tau_2$):** Executing more inter-node communications mitigates local drift and facilitates model consensus.
* **Network topology ($\zeta$):** A denser network topology accelerates model averaging and minimizes local drift, whereas sparser networks increase it. 

**V. Communication-Efficient DFL Based on Compressed Communication (C-DFL)**
To further boost communication efficiency, the authors apply a **gossip-based compressed communication scheme (CHOCO-G) into the DFL framework**. This allows the network to transmit compressed information (using techniques like sparsification or random quantization) to dramatically reduce communication payload. The authors provide a rigorous proof establishing that **C-DFL exhibits linear convergence for strongly convex objectives**. 

**VI. Simulation and Discussion**
The authors empirically evaluate DFL and C-DFL using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) on the MNIST and CIFAR-10 datasets over ring and quasi-ring network topologies. The experiments confirm the theoretical claims:
* **DFL accelerates convergence and improves testing accuracy** compared to C-SGD.
* Increasing communication frequency ($\tau_2$) improves performance, while excessive computation frequency ($\tau_1$) worsens it.
* **C-DFL successfully reduces communication overhead** and maintains strong convergence, provided the compression ratio is not set too small, which would result in excessive information loss.

**VII. Conclusion**
The authors summarize that the proposed DFL framework successfully **strikes a balance between communication efficiency and model consensus**. Both DFL and its compressed variant, C-DFL, possess strong theoretical convergence guarantees and demonstrate superior practical performance in experimental simulations.
